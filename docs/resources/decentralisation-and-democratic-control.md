# Building Decentralised AI: Democratic Alternatives

## Why this page exists

**SafeAI-Aus prepares Australia for AGI transformation through the [C·A·G·R framework](framework/index.md): Containment, Alignment, Governance, and Resilience.**

That framework addresses both catastrophic safety risks AND transformation governance challenges. Even if AGI is perfectly safe technically, profound questions remain: Who controls transformative technology? How are benefits distributed? Can democracies govern systems more capable than human experts?

**This page explores one approach to these governance challenges: decentralisation and democratic control.**

Most AGI discussion focuses on frontier lab safety, national AI strategies, or international governance coordination. Those are critical. But they don't address what happens when advanced AI is controlled by a small number of companies or governments—even if those systems are technically safe.

Decentralisation offers a complementary approach: distribute power across many actors, enable community ownership, and create alternatives to concentrated control. This page explains why decentralisation matters for AGI transformation and what it looks like in practice.

!!! tip "Decentralisation is one pathway, not the only pathway"

    The C·A·G·R framework requires multiple approaches working together:

    - **Frontier safety research** ensures advanced systems are technically safe
    - **International governance coordination** prevents dangerous races
    - **National policy frameworks** establish standards and oversight
    - **Decentralised alternatives** distribute power and enable democratic participation

    This page focuses on the last pathway because it's underrepresented in mainstream discourse—not because it's the only one that matters.

## The problem: Power concentration even with safe AGI

Even if AGI is perfectly safe and aligned, concentration of control in a few companies creates profound governance challenges. By 2032, Australian government and critical infrastructure could depend on a handful of foreign providers—giving them leverage over our laws, our data, and our sovereignty.

The AI systems work correctly. The problem is **who controls them, who benefits, and whose values they serve.**

## The d/acc approach: Differential acceleration toward defense and democracy

**This page follows the [d/acc (defensive/decentralisation/democratic accelerationism)](https://vitalik.eth.limo/general/2023/11/27/techno_optimism.html) framework from Vitalik Buterin.** We're pro-technology and pro-building—but we accelerate technologies that help society handle complexity, manage risk, and distribute benefits, rather than those that simply scale capability or concentrate power.

D/acc emphasizes four interconnected principles:

### 1. Differential acceleration

**Speed up what makes us resilient, not just what makes us capable.**

Instead of racing to build the most powerful centralized AI, accelerate:
- Technologies that help distributed actors coordinate
- Tools that improve robustness and recovery from failures
- Systems that distribute benefits broadly, not just to first movers
- Infrastructure that enables local agency and adaptation

**For AGI:** Build community AI infrastructure, open-source alternatives, and democratic governance tools *faster* than power concentration can solidify. The window is now—once dependencies deepen, building alternatives becomes exponentially harder.

### 2. Defensive advantage

**Make defense easier than attack; make open systems more defensible than closed ones.**

Decentralized and transparent systems have inherent defensive advantages:
- **Transparency as defense:** Many eyes can spot problems before they cascade
- **Local deployment:** No single chokepoint to compromise
- **Exit rights:** Users can leave systems that harm them, creating accountability
- **Redundancy:** Distributed systems survive individual node failures

**For AGI:** Open-source models enable independent security audits. Local deployment prevents foreign access to sensitive data. Community governance creates accountability through member oversight, not just distant regulation.

### 3. Decentralized systems

**Build infrastructure that doesn't rely on central chokepoints.**

When critical systems depend on a few providers, disruption (whether technical, commercial, or geopolitical) affects everyone. Decentralization ensures local actors can continue operating even when global consensus breaks down.

**For AGI:** Community-owned compute, open-source models, cooperative governance—ensuring no single point of failure and no concentration of power that creates strategic vulnerability.

### 4. Democratic participation

**Give more people real agency in shaping and benefiting from technological progress.**

Not just consultation after decisions are made—genuine democratic control through ownership, governance rights, and exit options. Communities decide how AI systems behave, what values they reflect, and who benefits.

**For AGI:** Platform cooperatives where members own and govern AI infrastructure. Participatory governance where affected users have real input. Benefit sharing, not extraction.

---

**D/acc is about building systems that maximize coordination while minimizing domination**—where risks are contained early and progress is shaped and accessible as broadly as possible.

### How d/acc supports C·A·G·R

D/acc isn't a replacement for the C·A·G·R framework—it's a set of principles for implementing parts of it:

- **[Containment](framework/containment.md):** Defensive advantage makes systems harder to compromise; distributed evaluation increases safety oversight
- **[Alignment](framework/alignment.md):** Democratic participation ensures we align with community values, not just corporate objectives
- **[Governance](framework/governance.md):** Decentralisation and democratic participation *are* governance mechanisms—distributing power rather than regulating concentrated power
- **[Resilience](framework/resilience.md):** All four d/acc principles build resilience through redundancy, transparency, exit rights, and distributed capability

**This page focuses on applying d/acc principles specifically to Governance and Resilience challenges in AGI transformation.**

## What decentralised AGI infrastructure looks like

Decentralisation isn't just theory. Real examples exist and are growing:

### Technical decentralisation: Open source and local deployment

**Open-source AI models** like Llama, Mistral, and others enable anyone with sufficient compute to run capable AI systems locally, without dependence on commercial APIs.

**Community model hubs** like Hugging Face provide infrastructure for sharing, evaluating, and deploying models democratically.

**Local deployment on commodity hardware** is increasingly viable as models become more efficient—reducing dependence on cloud providers.

→ [Open-Source Alternatives](business-industry/open-source-alternatives.md) for technical details

### Economic decentralisation: Cooperative ownership

**Platform cooperatives** apply democratic ownership to digital platforms. Members own and govern collectively, with profits distributed to users rather than extracted by shareholders.

**Community AI infrastructure** pools resources across multiple organizations for shared compute, models, and services—achieving scale economies while maintaining democratic control.

**Australian precedents:** We have strong cooperative traditions (credit unions, energy cooperatives, agricultural cooperatives) that can extend to AI infrastructure.

→ [Community AI Infrastructure](communities-households/community-ai-infrastructure.md) for implementation

### Governance decentralisation: Democratic control

**Participatory governance models** where users have genuine input on how AI systems behave, not just consultation after decisions are made.

**Subsidiarity:** Decisions made at the most local feasible level. Global coordination for global risks, community governance for local deployment.

**Real examples:**
- **Barcelona's digital democracy initiatives** (Decidim platform) - citizens participate in city governance through digital tools they collectively own
- **Taiwan's g0v movement** - civic technologists building open-source alternatives to government services
- **Mondragon Corporation** (Spain) - 80,000+ worker-owners demonstrating cooperative governance at scale

## Can decentralised approaches actually compete?

**The honest answer: Different tools for different purposes.**

Frontier centralized AI excels at some tasks: cutting-edge capabilities, massive computational resources, rapid iteration. Decentralized AI excels at others: **privacy, local customization, democratic governance, resilience, and trust.**

!!! success "We're early—and that's normal"

    **Open source started behind proprietary software.** Then it won on different dimensions: adaptability, security through transparency, longevity, community innovation.

    **Decentralized systems often start behind centralized ones.** Then they win by being:

    - **More trustworthy** - transparent governance, no hidden agendas
    - **More adaptable** - local customization for specific contexts
    - **More resilient** - distributed systems survive individual node failures
    - **More legitimate** - democratic control by affected users

    The question isn't "can decentralized AI match GPT-6?" It's **"what can decentralized AI become that centralized can't?"**

### What decentralised AI can do that centralised can't

**Operate during disruption:** Local systems work when cloud services fail or are cut off geopolitically.

**Reflect local values:** Communities govern systems to match their values, not distant corporate objectives.

**Maintain genuine privacy:** Data never leaves local infrastructure—true data sovereignty.

**Enable exit rights:** Users unsatisfied with one provider can switch to community alternatives without losing everything.

**Experiment with governance:** Different communities try different democratic AI governance models, learning what works.

### Strategic complementarity: We need both

This isn't either/or. We need:
- **Frontier centralized AI** for cutting-edge capabilities, large-scale coordination
- **Decentralized alternatives** for resilience, democracy, privacy, local adaptation

**The danger is monoculture.** If only centralized approaches exist, there's no accountability, no alternatives when things go wrong, no democratic participation. Decentralization provides the competitive pressure that makes centralized providers accountable.

## Theory of change: How do we get there?

Building decentralised alternatives isn't about replacing OpenAI tomorrow. It's about creating **negotiating power through exit rights** and **demonstrating what's possible.**

### Phase 1: Prove viability (now - 2027)

**Pilots and experiments:**
- Community organizations deploy local AI for specific use cases
- Cooperatives form to share compute and models
- Government pilots procurement from community providers
- Demonstrate that decentralised approaches work

**Build capability:**
- Train people in open-source AI deployment
- Develop governance frameworks for democratic AI
- Create legal structures for cooperative AI ownership
- Establish evaluation methods for community deployment

### Phase 2: Scale and legitimacy (2027-2030)

**Proven alternatives create negotiating power:**
- Businesses can credibly threaten exit from extractive platforms
- Government procurement diversifies away from single providers
- Communities operate successful AI infrastructure independently
- **Exit rights make voice more powerful**

**Network effects shift:**
- More developers contribute to open ecosystems
- More organizations join cooperatives for scale
- Standards emerge for interoperability
- Decentralized options become default for certain use cases

### Phase 3: Distributed ecosystem (2030+)

**Multiple viable governance models:**
- Some services centralized (appropriate for coordination)
- Some decentralized (appropriate for local control)
- Some cooperative (appropriate for shared resources)
- **Competition ensures accountability across all models**

**Democratic participation normalized:**
- Users expect governance rights in AI systems they depend on
- Community ownership common for critical infrastructure
- Open source default for public sector
- **Power distributed, not concentrated**

## The urgency: Why act now

**Windows close quickly in technology:**

Once a few providers become dominant and lock-in deepens, building alternatives becomes exponentially harder. Network effects compound. Standards ossify around incumbent choices. Dependencies become too deep to unwind.

**We're still early enough to shape the landscape:**

- Open-source AI models are competitive for many use cases
- Community infrastructure experiments are viable
- Lock-in hasn't fully solidified
- Regulatory frameworks are still forming

**The next 3-5 years determine the architecture:**

Will AGI transformation happen through:
- **Concentrated control** by a few tech giants and governments, or
- **Distributed power** with genuine alternatives and democratic participation?

**By the time concentration is complete, building alternatives may be impossible.** Now is the time to build.

## Common questions

**"Won't market competition solve this? Why does government need to intervene?"**

Market forces created the concentration—they won't fix it. Training frontier AI requires billions in capital. Network effects and first-mover advantages create winner-take-all dynamics. Without intervention, we get oligopoly, not competition. Strategic sectors require active governance to maintain alternatives.

**"Won't this slow down AI development?"**

Some centralized development may be faster initially. But monoculture is fragile. Diverse approaches increase robustness and enable experimentation. The strategic question is: **What speed, for whom, toward what ends?** Democratic governance ensures development serves broad interests, not just first-mover advantage.

**"Can cooperatives really compete with Big Tech?"**

In some domains, absolutely—privacy, local customization, trust, democratic values. In others, they start behind but catch up (see: open source software). The point isn't replacing all centralized AI with cooperatives—it's ensuring **alternatives exist so users have genuine choice and providers face accountability.**

**"Is Australia too small for this?"**

Alone, yes—achieving scale economies is challenging. But **regional cooperation** (Pacific, Southeast Asia) creates sufficient scale while maintaining democratic governance. Australia's cooperative tradition and research capability position us well to lead regional initiatives.
